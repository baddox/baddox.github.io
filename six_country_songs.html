How Similar Are Those Six Country Songs?

A recently popular mashup of six country songs purports to show how similar they are. Let's have a listen and find out how similar they really are.

The Songs

  1. "Sure Be Cool If You Did"—Blake Shelton
  2. "Drunk on You"—Luke Bryan
  3. "Chillin' It"—Cole Swindell
  4. "Close Your Eyes"—Parmalee
  5. "This is How We Roll"—Florida Georgia Line
  6. "Ready, Set, Roll"—Chase Rice

The Chord Progressions

Clearly, the idea behind the mashup is that these songs all have the same chord progression throughout. That progression, in Roman numeral analysis, is VI I V V. Let's compare that to the various sections of each song. Each column in the table represents one measure of music.

Introduction

Song M1 M2 M3 M4
Mashup IV I V V
1 I/3 IV - I/3 V V
2 IV I V vi
3 IV I V V
4 IV I V V
5 IV I V V
6 vi IV I V

Alright, those introduction are pretty good matches. Weirdly, song 1 plays at the very beginning of the mashup, even though its chord progression doesn't match the rest of the mashup. Song 6 stands out like a sore thumb in this table, but Sir Mashalot cleverly hid it behind song 5. That was easy, because the introduction in song 6 is just a single synth tone playing the chord roots in eight notes. Song 6 is nearly inaudible, and its lack of polyphony would make the difference in chord progression overlookable.

Verse

Song M1 M2 M3 M4
Mashup IV I V V
1 I/3 IV V V
2 vi I V I
3 IV I V vi - V
4 IV vi V V
5 IV I V V
6 vi IV I V

Things are much less similar in the opening progression of the verse (stay tuned for further verse developments). Only song 5 gets a perfect match (spoiler alert: song 5 matches all the way through). Songs 2 and 4 have a significantly different sound to my ears, because of that minor chord and its effect on the vocal melody. Song 6 continues to have very little similarity, but it would fit right in the other popular mashup about shared chord progressions.

Chorus

Song M1 M2 M3 M4
Mashup IV I V V
1 IV I V V
2 IV I V vi
3 IV I V vi - V
4 IV I vi V
5 IV I V V
6 IV I V vi

This is uncanny. My guess is that the similarity of these choruses is what initially gave Sir Mashalot the idea. To me, the minor chord in the last measure doesn't significantly change the sound of the progression. You can even hear the minor chord prominently at 3:57 in the mashup, under the lyrics "sun comes up" from song 6. Only the progression in song 4 sounds significantly different.

Even More Progressions

My chord charts have only documented the first four measures of each song section, because the mashup uses just that one 4-measure VI I V V progression throughout. Some of the songs use different progressions in other sections of the song. Listen for these yourself if you dare. One good thing about modern pop country is that it's generally quite easy to discern the chords. It's not pop punk easy, but it's close. Song 2 even sneaks in a 2-bar progression which might for one instant disorient you rhythmically.

Notes

I'm representing slash chords with the Roman numeral indicating the chord itself, and the Arabic numeral after the slash representing the bass note as a scale degree relative to the root of the chord. Thus, in the key of C, a I/3 chord is a C major chord with an E in the bass, and a V/3 chord is a G major chord with a B in the bass. I wasn't able to find any agreed-upon notation for slash chords in Roman numeral analysis.
